Comparison · Choice · What Remains

A
Albright Ai Short Fiction
18 min read

When Daniel was seventeen, his grandmother gave him a wooden bowl.

It wasn't valuable. It wasn't antique. It wasn't even particularly beautiful. One side was slightly warped, and the varnish had faded into uneven shades of brown.

"Why this?" Daniel asked.

His grandmother smiled.

"Because one day you'll understand."

At seventeen, Daniel considered this answer deeply unsatisfying.

At twenty-seven, he still didn't understand.

The bowl sat on a shelf in his apartment overlooking the city, gathering dust while his life unfolded through a six-inch screen.

Every morning began the same way.

Before his feet touched the floor, he opened Instagram.

Someone was vacationing in Bali.

Someone had launched a startup.

Someone had gotten engaged.

Someone had bought a Mercedes.

Someone was six-pack shredded.

Someone had just moved to Dubai.

Someone had married someone stunning.

Someone seemed impossibly happy.

The feed never ended.

Neither did the comparisons.

Daniel wasn't unsuccessful. He had a decent marketing job. Good friends. Reasonable health. A clean apartment.

Yet every day felt like evidence that he was choosing his life poorly.

Because there were too many lives to compare it against.

The internet had expanded his world beyond anything his parents could have imagined.

His father had grown up in a small town.

When his father was young, the prettiest girl he knew was the prettiest girl he knew. The richest man he knew was the richest man he knew. The smartest person he knew was the smartest person he knew.

Comparison had boundaries.

Daniel's generation had none.

Every day he encountered thousands of people.

Models. Millionaires. Athletes. Entrepreneurs. Digital nomads. Influencers.

People who woke up at 4 AM. People who retired at 30. People who earned six figures. People who earned seven.

People who seemed to have solved existence itself.

The result wasn't inspiration.

It was paralysis.

Every path looked inferior to another path. Every choice seemed wrong.

One Friday evening, Daniel met his friend Jamal for coffee.

Jamal had known him since university and possessed the rare ability to detect dissatisfaction before Daniel voiced it.

"You've been quiet."

"I'm thinking."

"Dangerous."

Daniel laughed.

"I don't know what I'm doing with my life."

"Again?"

Daniel rolled his eyes. "Seriously."

Jamal leaned back. "What's wrong now?"

Daniel hesitated. "Maybe I should leave marketing."

"For what?" "Tech." "Why?" "Better money." "Then do it." "But maybe AI will replace those jobs." "Then don't." "Maybe I should start a business." "Doing what?" "I don't know."

Jamal stared at him. "You know your problem?"

"I assume you're about to tell me."

"You think every decision is permanent."

Daniel shrugged. "Some are." "Not most."

Jamal pointed toward the window. Outside, hundreds of people moved through the city.

"Look at them." Daniel looked. "What about them?"

"Every one of them thinks somebody else has figured life out."

A month later, Daniel attended a networking event.

He hated networking events. They were performance theaters disguised as conversations.

Everyone presented polished versions of themselves.

Nobody discussed anxiety. Nobody discussed confusion. Nobody discussed loneliness.

Everyone sounded successful. Everyone sounded certain.

Daniel found himself speaking with a woman named Amara.

She was beautiful. Not merely physically attractive. Confident. Thoughtful. Grounded. The kind of presence that made other people seem blurry.

They spoke for nearly an hour. Books. Travel. Family. Work. Music. Life.

When she laughed, he found himself wanting to hear it again.

As the evening ended, they exchanged numbers.

Walking home, Daniel felt strangely hopeful.

Then he opened social media. A mistake.

Within minutes he was comparing himself against hundreds of men. Men richer than him. Taller than him. Fitter than him. More successful than him.

The optimism vanished.

By midnight he had convinced himself Amara could date virtually anyone. Why would she choose him?

He never texted her.

Three months later, he saw a photo online. She was engaged.

Not to one of the imaginary competitors in his head. To a teacher. A completely ordinary teacher.

Daniel stared at the picture. The teacher wasn't exceptionally handsome. Wasn't wealthy. Wasn't famous.

He had simply made a choice.

He had acted while Daniel compared.

Years passed. The world became more connected. And somehow more fragmented.

Algorithms learned exactly what people desired. Then showed them people who possessed it. If someone wanted beauty, endless beauty appeared. If someone wanted wealth, endless wealth appeared.

The machine generated dissatisfaction at industrial scale.

Daniel wasn't immune.

Dating apps became another battlefield. Thousands of profiles. Thousands of possibilities. Thousands of alternatives.

Every match competed against hypothetical future matches. Every conversation competed against imagined conversations. Everyone became replaceable.

One evening he sat across from a woman named Sophia at dinner. She was intelligent. Funny. Kind. The date was genuinely enjoyable.

But afterward his brain whispered:

What if someone better exists? What if the next swipe is perfect? What if you're settling? What if she's settling?

The relationship lasted six weeks. Not because of incompatibility. Because uncertainty consumed possibility.

Years later he couldn't remember why they stopped seeing each other. Only that they did.

At thirty-two, Daniel received news that his grandmother had died.

The funeral took place in the town where she'd lived for nearly sixty years.

Afterward, family members sorted through her belongings. Daniel wandered through her house.

Everything felt smaller than he remembered. The kitchen. The hallway. The living room.

Then he noticed something. The wooden bowl. An almost identical bowl sat on her dining table.

He picked it up. His aunt noticed. "She loved those." "Why?"

His aunt smiled. "Did she never tell you?" "No."

"Those bowls were handmade by your grandfather."

Daniel froze. His grandfather had died before Daniel was born.

"He made hundreds." "Hundreds?" His aunt nodded.

"He tried different woods. Different shapes. Different finishes." She ran her fingers across the bowl. "But this one was her favorite."

Daniel examined it. It wasn't perfect. Actually, several others nearby looked better. More symmetrical. Better crafted. More polished.

"Why this one?" His aunt laughed. "Because it was the one she chose."

Daniel frowned. "That's it?" "That's it."

That night he stayed in her empty house.

Unable to sleep, he wandered through old photo albums. Black-and-white pictures. Faded memories. Lives compressed into pages.

His grandparents appeared again and again. Not glamorous. Not rich. Not extraordinary by modern standards. Yet they looked happy.

Genuinely happy. Not performatively happy. Not algorithmically happy. Happy.

Daniel noticed something else. Their lives were remarkably limited.

They had never traveled internationally. Never browsed dating apps. Never compared themselves against celebrities. Never consumed endless streams of curated perfection.

Their world was smaller. Their commitments stronger. Their choices fewer. And perhaps because of that, their gratitude deeper.

Around midnight he found a handwritten note tucked between photographs. His grandmother's handwriting.

The note wasn't addressed to anyone. Maybe it was a journal entry. Maybe a reminder. Maybe both.

It read:

"The secret isn't finding the best thing. The secret is loving what you've chosen. There will always be something prettier, richer, newer, smarter, easier. If you spend your life searching for the perfect option, you'll never belong anywhere. Every meaningful thing becomes meaningful because you stay."

Daniel stared at the words. Again. And again. And again.

Something shifted.

The next morning he sat on the porch watching the sunrise.

For years he had assumed freedom meant unlimited options. Unlimited options meant unlimited potential. Unlimited potential meant happiness.

Yet his actual experience suggested the opposite.

The more options he gained, the harder decisions became. The harder decisions became, the less satisfied he felt.

Choice promised freedom. Excessive choice produced imprisonment.

A paradox.

He suddenly understood the bowl.

His grandmother could have preferred hundreds of others. Better bowls existed. More beautiful bowls existed. More valuable bowls existed.

That wasn't the point.

Meaning emerged from commitment. Not optimization.

One afternoon he attended a community event. A cultural festival celebrating dozens of backgrounds and traditions.

Music drifted through the air. Children ran between booths. Languages intertwined like rivers.

Daniel found himself speaking with a woman named Zuri.

She had grown up in a different country. Different culture. Different experiences. Different assumptions about life.

Years earlier those differences might have intimidated him. Now they fascinated him.

They spent hours talking. Not comparing. Not performing. Not evaluating alternatives. Simply exploring another human being.

Over time they grew closer. Friends first. Then something more.

People occasionally questioned their relationship. Different races. Different backgrounds. Different families. Different histories.

But Daniel noticed something. The strongest connections weren't built from similarity. They were built from curiosity.

Comparison creates distance. Curiosity creates intimacy.

One asks: "How do you rank against others?" The other asks: "Who are you?"

Several years later, Daniel sat in his apartment. A different apartment now. Larger. Warmer. Filled with evidence of an actual life.

Books. Photographs. Plants. Memories.

Zuri was reading nearby. Rain tapped gently against the windows. The wooden bowl sat on a shelf. Still imperfect. Still slightly warped. Still his favorite object.

His phone buzzed. A notification. Someone announcing success. Someone traveling somewhere exotic. Someone achieving something impressive.

For a moment the old reflex appeared. Comparison. The familiar temptation.

Then it faded.

Because he finally understood something social media could never teach.

Every person online represented a path not taken. And there were billions of them.

You could spend an entire lifetime examining those paths. Comparing. Evaluating. Wondering. Optimizing. Scrolling.

Or you could live one.

Not the perfect one. Not the objectively best one. Just yours.

Daniel placed the phone face down.

Outside, the rain continued. Inside, Zuri smiled at something in her book. The bowl remained quietly on its shelf.

And for the first time in many years, Daniel felt no urgency to be somewhere else, someone else, or living some other life.

The world still contained infinite possibilities.

But happiness, he discovered, had never lived among the possibilities.

It lived in the choice.

The choice

You could spend an entire lifetime examining paths not taken. Or you could live one. Not the perfect one. Not the objectively best one. Just yours.
